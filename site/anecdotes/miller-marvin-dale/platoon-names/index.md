archivist_notes: |
  On what Marvin chose to say: Marvin did not volunteer these facts unprompted. They emerged because his son expressed a preference — first for Skull, then for Range — and Marvin corrected each one with a single sentence. He was not telling stories. He was closing doors, gently, in a way a child might not fully register as such.

  The two statements are among the most compressed pieces of testimony in this archive. Each one carries an enormous weight of event, loss, and personal proximity in a single clause. He said them flatly, without elaboration, and stopped.

  On the timing: Marvin knew about the Skull Platoon crash despite it occurring after his departure from Vietnam. This means information reached him through postwar contact — most likely through Larry Cate, his only sustained postwar friendship from the unit. The fact that he carried this knowledge, and that it came to mind immediately when his son said he would have chosen Skull, suggests it weighed on him.

  On April 20, 1971: Marvin's characterization of Range Platoon as having been "wiped out by an ambush" is the only statement he ever made to his son about the April 20 engagement. He was in the unit when it happened. The archive contains other accounts of that day from other sources. His own words about it are these two sentences, spoken to a child, and not repeated.

  On the missed opportunity: His son's reflection — that he may have inadvertently closed a conversation that could have opened further — is a common experience among the children of veterans, and worth preserving as plainly as he expressed it. It is not guilt; it is the particular grief of realizing, too late, that a door existed. The archive itself is one answer to that grief: the stories are being found by other means. But this one — whatever else Marvin might have said that day if the conversation had turned differently — is gone.

D Company, 2/8 Cav had three platoons: Cat, Range, and Skull. Marvin Miller's platoon was Cat.

He was explaining this to his son — who was ten or twelve years old at the time, somewhere in the 1980s — when his son said that Skull sounded the coolest, and that he would have chosen Skull.

Marvin said: Skull was decimated by a helicopter crash.

He did not describe the crash further. He did not give a date, name casualties, or elaborate.

His son said he would have chosen Range instead.

Marvin said: Range was wiped out by an ambush.

He said nothing further. The conversation ended there.

---

Marvin was referring to real events. The Skull Platoon crash occurred on May 10, 1972 — after his discharge, effective December 2, 1971. He knew about it, which means news of it reached him after his return, likely through Larry Cate. The ambush he described was the engagement of April 20, 1971 — an event documented elsewhere in this archive through other accounts. He was in the unit when it happened.

His son's recollection, recorded here in his own words:

*Looking back, I wonder if I offended him a little by suggesting I would have wanted to be in the other platoons — the ones that were gone. And in doing so, I may have missed my only opportunity to learn more. It turned out to be exactly that: the only opportunity. He never came closer to the subject again.*
