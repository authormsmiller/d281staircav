# Event — Contact, October 21–22, 1971
## Archive ID: MDM-EVENT-19711021
## Status: PARTIAL — Kutter written account not yet transcribed
## Compiled: April 2026

---

### Summary

On the evening of October 21, 1971, Delta Company, 2/8 Cav sustained
a contact in which PFC Bill Makowski (Range 71) was fatally wounded.
The contact occurred near FSB Jeffries, Long Khanh Province. Due to
darkness and the refusal of a medevac pilot to fly into the combat zone,
the Battalion Commander personally flew his own helicopter to extract
the wounded. The following morning — October 22 — the area was subjected
to coordinated artillery and air strikes, then patrolled by the unit to
confirm the enemy had been cleared.

### The Contact — Evening of October 21, 1971

The contact occurred in the evening. Makowski was seriously wounded —
the nature of the wound and exact circumstances of the ambush are to be
confirmed from Kutter's written account.

**Cat Platoon's position:** Cat Platoon was near Captain Kutter and the
command post during this contact and was not in a forward position to
address the ambush — consistent with the pattern observed on April 20,
1971. (See MDM-ACCOUNT-KUTTER-OCT21 for analysis of this pattern.)

**The medevac refusal and Stone Mountain 6:**
Once Makowski had been moved to a safer position, darkness had fallen.
The medevac helicopter pilot refused to fly into the combat zone at night
to extract the wounded.

"Stone Mountain 6" — the Battalion Commander's call sign — personally
took his own helicopter into the combat zone to extract the wounded rather
than leave Makowski in the field overnight.

The Battalion Commander subsequently ensured that the pilot who refused
the medevac received the worst possible assignment available to him. The
specific nature of that reassignment is not recorded in this account.

**Note on Stone Mountain 6:** The Battalion Commander's call sign was
"Stone Mountain 6." This has been confirmed as **LTC Thomas Elwood Blagg**,
commanding officer of 2nd Battalion, 8th Cavalry, 1st Cavalry Division,
1971. Blagg's background is directly relevant to his decision to fly
personally to extract the wounded:

In 1964 as a Special Forces captain, Blagg had strapped his badly wounded
interpreter to his back and continued fighting after his own arm was
shattered, refusing evacuation until the battle was won — earning his
Silver Star. This was a man with a documented record of never leaving
wounded behind. His decision to fly his personal helicopter into a night
combat zone on October 21, 1971 rather than wait until morning is entirely
consistent with his character and history.

Blagg subsequently became friends with Colin Powell and commanded brigades
alongside him in the 101st Airborne Division in the 1970s. He was recognized
by the Angry Skipper Association in the January 2007 newsletter ("Col. Blagg
Silver Star" story). He passed away in 2023.

Sources: Encyclopedia of Arkansas (encyclopediaofarkansas.net);
Arkansas State University Hall of Heroes citation for Silver Star, 1964.

**Note on Makowski's evacuation:** Despite the Battalion Commander's
personal intervention to extract him, Bill Makowski did not survive.
He died of his wounds. His age: 20 years old, having just had a birthday.
He had been in the field only one month.

---

### The Follow-Up — Morning of October 22, 1971

Per Captain Kutter's account: the following morning, October 22, the
area where the ambush occurred was subjected to coordinated artillery
fire and aerial bombardment — "blown off the face of the Earth" in
Kutter's characterization.

Following the bombardment, the unit patrolled into the area to confirm
it was clear of enemy forces. This patrol accounts for McGrew's second
"Contact" entry on October 22 — it was not necessarily a new engagement
but the follow-up sweep of the ambush site after the bombardment.

The unit returned to FSB Jeffries on October 23 (confirmed by McGrew
calendar: "Back to Jeff").

### Makowski — Personal Details

- **Name:** William (Bill) Makowski
- **Unit:** Range Platoon, D/2-8 Cav, 1971
- **Birthday:** October 16, 1951 — turned 20 years old five days before
  his death
- **Date of death:** October 21, 1971
- **Time with unit:** Approximately one month — joined D Company in
  September 1971
- **Role at time of contact:** M60 machine gunner — a position he
  volunteered for. The M60 gunner was typically out front, one of the
  more exposed positions in an infantry formation, and therefore one
  of the more dangerous roles to fill.
- **Character:** Well-liked by the Range Platoon men who served with him.
  Despite being new to the unit, he had made an impression quickly enough
  that veterans remembered him specifically and honored him decades later.
- **FSB named in his honor:** FSB Makowski
- **Premonition:** Per the 2022 newsletter, Makowski had left for Vietnam
  "with the strong premonition that he would not be coming back alive."
- **Memorial:** October 21, 2021 — 50th anniversary memorial, Forest Lawn
  Cemetery, Buffalo NY. Attended by Wolf Kutter, Doug Hilts, Dizzy Blais,
  Kirk Davis, Jim and Susan Garvin. WIVB News 4 Buffalo covered the event.
  Approximately 40 people attended.
- **Family:** Barbara Hilts handmade a memorial quilt for the Makowski
  family. Doug Hilts presented the quilt. Kirk Davis created a memorial
  poster.

### Others Wounded — October 21, 1971

The ambush involved enemy fire followed by Chicom claymore mines. Makowski,
on the M60, took the worst of both.

**Doug Hilts** (Range 70-71): A bullet passed through his helmet during
the contact. Hilts did not notice it at the time — the wound was not
serious and the chaos of the ambush and its aftermath occupied his
attention. The bullet-shaped hole in his helmet was discovered by
another soldier the following morning, October 22, when the unit
patrolled back into the area after the bombardment. Hilts reflected
on it without much weight at the time.

**Dizzy Blais** (Range 71): Suffered shrapnel wounds to his face during
the ambush, described as moderate. He was treated and returned to duty.

**What neither man knew on October 21, 1971:**

More than twenty years later, Dizzy Blais was at a dentist's appointment
requiring an X-ray. The X-ray revealed a piece of metal lodged in his jaw
— unfelt and unknown for over two decades. When examined, it was identified
as a fragment of Doug Hilts' helmet.

The bullet that passed through Hilts' helmet had not stopped there. It
had carried a fragment of the helmet's material forward and embedded it
in Blais' jaw, where it remained for more than twenty years, causing no
symptoms, discovered only by chance.

Three men in the same ambush on the evening of October 21, 1971:
- Bill Makowski — killed by enemy fire and Chicom claymore
- Doug Hilts — bullet through his helmet, wound not serious, hole
  found the next morning
- Dizzy Blais — shrapnel to the face, and a piece of Hilts' helmet
  in his jaw for twenty years

The physical distance between all three outcomes — death, a perforated
helmet, a hidden fragment — was measured in feet. The temporal distance
between the moment and its full discovery was more than two decades.

**Source of this account:** Doug Hilts, told directly to Marvin Miller's
son. Hilts was present for both the October 21 contact and the discovery
of the helmet hole the following morning. The dental discovery was related
to Hilts by Blais and passed on by Hilts in the same account.

### Significance for Marvin Miller's Timeline

October 21, 1971 was likely among Marvin's last field operations before
being stood down as a short-timer (DEROS December 2). The short-timer
policy confirmed by Kutter and Romani pulled men off operations
approximately one month before DEROS — placing Marvin's stand-down
around early November. October 21 may have been his last time in the bush.

Cat Platoon's position near the CP during this contact — rather than in
the forward elements — is consistent with Kutter's management of short-
timers and experienced NCOs as his DEROS approached.
