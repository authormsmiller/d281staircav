### Short-Timer Policy — Confirmed by Kutter and Romani

Both Captain Wolf Kutter and Val Romani have confirmed that soldiers who
were "short" — approaching their DEROS date — were not sent to the bush.
They were kept at Battalion HQ for the final period of their tour.

**Implications for Marvin's timeline:**
Marvin's DEROS was December 2, 1971. If the short-timer policy pulled
men off operations approximately one month before DEROS, he would have
come off the line in approximately late October or early November 1971.

The October 21 contact in which Makowski was killed — if it was near
the end of Marvin's operational period — may have been one of his last
patrols, possibly the last. This is consistent with:

- His positioning near the CP with Kutter on October 21 (a squad leader
  being kept closer to command as his DEROS approached)
- The tone of the August 24 and September 3 letters ("not too busy,"
  "the days keep chugging by")
- The November 24 letter's explicit description of a "short-timer's
  attitude" and request that his family stop writing
- The general boredom and administrative quality of his final weeks,
  which now reads as Battalion HQ duty rather than field operations

**What the short-timer policy means for the archive:**
Marvin's final month in-country was spent at Battalion HQ, not in the
field. His comments about boredom and short-timer psychology were not
just emotional states — they were the literal reality of a soldier
who had been stood down from operations and was waiting out his
remaining days in an administrative role.

The contrast with the rest of his tour — the April 20 contact, the
Huey crash, the Chieu Hoi escort, the M60 incident, the patrols from
half a dozen FSBs — makes the final quiet weeks feel earned rather
than anticlimactic. He had done the work. The Army was keeping him
alive to go home.

**The 2022 ASA Newsletter (MDM-NEWSLETTER-2022-JAN)** provides Jim
Garvin's account of the memorial events surrounding Makowski's death,
but limited tactical detail about the contact itself. The following is
confirmed from that source:

- Bill Makowski, Range 71, was killed October 21, 1971
- He had been in the field only one month and had just turned 20
- Doug Hilts later realized a bullet had passed through his helmet lining
  during the same firefight, leaving him stunned with limited memory
- Dizzy Blais sustained shrapnel wounds to his face
- Wolf Kutter attended the 50th anniversary memorial on October 21, 2021
  in Buffalo, NY and delivered the graveside tribute
- Kutter's wife Tracie was still recovering from surgery but they made
  the four-day journey to honor Makowski

**From Marvin Miller's son (April 2026):** Cat Platoon was near Captain
Wolf Kutter and the CP (command post) during the October 21 contact and
was not in a position to address the ambush that killed Makowski. This
is consistent with a pattern observed across two incidents:

- April 20, 1971: Cat Platoon on south bank, Range on north bank leading
  contact. Cat not in effective supporting position (terrain geometry,
  elevation differential — per Weaver account).
- October 21, 1971: Cat Platoon near CP with Kutter, not forward.

### Analytical Note — Cat Platoon's Tactical Role

Two incidents in which contacts resulted in KIAs, and in both cases Cat
Platoon was with the CP rather than in the forward assault or flanking
position. This pattern warrants examination when Kutter's full account
is available.

Possible explanations:

**1. CP security assignment.** In standard infantry company operations,
one platoon was often assigned to travel with and protect the company
commander and command post — serving as a reserve and command security
element. If Cat was consistently assigned this role during 1971, it
would explain their positioning in both incidents.

**2. Reconnaissance specialization.** Cat Platoon soldiers carried MOS
11F (Infantry Recon). Recon elements typically screened, gathered
intelligence, and reported rather than leading assaults. Their tactical
employment would naturally differ from a straight infantry platoon like
Range.

**3. Leadership reliability.** The April 20 account (Weaver) suggests
Cat's patrol leadership exercised sound tactical judgment in not firing
under conditions that would have endangered Range. A platoon known for
disciplined judgment might be specifically assigned to the CP security
role precisely because they could be trusted not to create incidents.

**4. Unit rotation and timing.** October 21 was during Marvin's
post-promotion squad leader period (E-5 from August 4). It is worth
establishing where specifically within the CP/Kutter formation Marvin's
squad was positioned, and whether he had any role in the response.

These questions should be put directly to Kutter when contact is made,
and cross-referenced against his written account when it becomes
accessible.
