# Event Record — The Bee Sting Incident
## Archive ID: MDM-EVENT-BEES
## Sources: Multiple (see below)
## Recorded: Conversations with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025
## Date of incident: March 21, 1971

---

### Sources

Multiple sources have described this incident, with some variation in detail. No single account is identified as definitive. Sources include:

- **Capt. Bill Neal** — CO, D/2-8 Cav at the time of Neal's arrival (~April 13); had recollections of the incident and its consequences for the command. Account shared in conversation with Marvin's son, 2025.
- **Howard McGrew** — Range Platoon CP radio operator; one of the soldiers medevaced for treatment. Calendar entry for March 21, 1971 reads: "Medevac — Bee Stings — to FB Mace." Shared calendar and recollections with Marvin's son.
- **Additional unnamed soldiers** — at least two other accounts of the incident have been heard by Marvin's son, one attributing the trigger to soldiers deliberately disturbing a nest, one attributing it to accidental contact with a rotted tree trunk. Neither source is named in this document.

---

### The Incident

On or around March 21, 1971, while D Company was operating in the field, a large and aggressive bee colony was disturbed. The exact trigger is disputed across sources:

- **Account A:** Soldiers were "messing around" with what was clearly a bee's nest — the disturbance was deliberate, if not malicious.
- **Account B:** A soldier leaned against a rotted tree trunk that contained the nest — the contact was accidental.

In either case, the bees — described as large and highly aggressive — attacked. The reaction was immediate and severe enough that men ran from the attack **without their weapons**, shouting. The loss of weapons discipline in a combat zone is itself a measure of how sudden and overwhelming the attack was.

Multiple soldiers were stung badly enough to require medical evacuation to FSB Mace for treatment. Among those medevaced were **Howard McGrew** and, based on accounts from Neal and others, **Capt. Jim Bedsole** — the company commander at the time.

---

### Command Consequences

Capt. Bedsole's reaction to the stings was severe enough that he could not continue in command. Based on recollections from Capt. Neal and others, the bee sting incident is believed to have been the direct cause — or a primary contributing cause — of Bedsole's relief from command and Neal's subsequent arrival as CO on approximately April 13, 1971.

The approximately three-week gap between the March 21 incident and Neal's April 13 arrival is consistent with the time required to arrange an emergency replacement commanding officer. Marvin's letter home around that date described the arrival of "a new captain that doesn't give them much time off" — written without knowledge of, or at least without comment on, the circumstances that had necessitated the change.

This has not yet been formally confirmed with Neal as a direct causal account. It remains probable but unverified. See Open Questions.

---

### On the Bees

The species of bee has not been confirmed. Based on the description — large, highly aggressive, colony nesting in or near a rotted tree trunk, capable of producing a mass attack severe enough to require multiple medevacs — the most probable candidate is the **Asian giant honey bee** (*Apis dorsata* or a related species), which is native to Vietnam and known for aggressive defensive behavior when its colony is disturbed. Vietnamese jungle environments in 1971 would have been consistent with this species' habitat.

This is an inference, not a confirmed identification. Marvin's own field observations (MDM-FIELDNOTES-BUSH) note that centipedes were what scared the local Vietnamese people most — he did not specifically mention bees. The absence of a mention may simply reflect that this particular incident did not involve him directly, or that he did not witness it firsthand.

---

### Archivist Notes

**On the weapons detail:**
The image of soldiers running from a bee attack without their weapons is striking precisely because of its context. These were men trained to maintain weapons discipline under fire. That the bees produced a reaction indistinguishable in its effect from a rout — men running, abandoning equipment, shouting — says something about the severity of the attack that a body count of medevacs alone does not convey.

**On the two trigger accounts:**
Both accounts are preserved here without adjudication. "Messing around with a nest" and "leaned against a rotted trunk" are not necessarily incompatible — one may describe the first soldier's action, the other a second contact. Or they may simply be the difference between what two observers saw from different positions. Neither account affects the consequences of the incident.

**On McGrew as source:**
McGrew's calendar entry is terse — "Medevac — Bee Stings — to FB Mace" — but he was personally medevaced, which means he recorded an event he was in the middle of. The entry is one of the more personally significant ones in the calendar, written by a man who was himself a casualty of the incident.

**On the command change:**
If the bee sting incident directly caused Bedsole's relief from command, it is one of the more unusual command changes in the unit's record — a company CO removed not by combat, not by rotation, but by insects in a rotted tree. It would also mean that the entire final eight months of Marvin's tour — under Neal and then Kutter — were shaped in part by what happened on a jungle trail on March 21, 1971.
