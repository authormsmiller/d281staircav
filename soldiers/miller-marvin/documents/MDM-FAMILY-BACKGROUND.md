## The Miller Family — Kittanning, Armstrong County, Pennsylvania

### Family Structure

Marvin Dale Miller (b. September 15, 1949) was the seventh of eight children
born to his parents in Kittanning, Armstrong County, Pennsylvania. The
children in birth order:

1. **Don Miller** ("Buss") — oldest brother
2. **Evelyn Miller**
3. **Pat Miller**
4. **Carolyn Miller**
5. **Mary Ellen Miller**
6. **Dan Miller** — older brother, closest to Marvin in age
7. **Marvin Dale Miller** — subject of this archive
8. **Judy Miller** — youngest child

Parents' names: to be confirmed and added.

### The House Fire — 1960

Shortly after Marvin's 10th birthday in 1959 (he was born September 15,
1949, so this would place the fire in late 1959 or 1960), the family home
burned down. Marvin would have been approximately 10 years old.

The family rebuilt incrementally over the following decade, but the home
remained under construction or unfinished for years afterward. Construction
work on the family home is referenced in Marvin's letters from Vietnam
(1970–1971) as one of the purposes for which his pay allotment was being
used — meaning the house was still being completed more than ten years
after the fire.

This is the foundational fact of the family's financial situation during
Marvin's childhood and early adulthood. Eight children, a burned home,
a decade of reconstruction in a working-class Pennsylvania community.

### Financial Situation During Marvin's Vietnam Service (1970–1971)

The Miller family was by all accounts a poor family. During Marvin's tour,
his Army pay represented a meaningful contribution to the household.

**Marvin's pay arrangement:** He kept approximately $10 per month for
personal use and sent the remainder home to his mother, who managed the
funds. An E-4 infantryman in Vietnam in 1971 earned approximately $350–400
per month including combat pay and overseas allowances. Marvin was therefore
sending home approximately $340 per month — consistently, for the duration
of his tour.

**Known uses of the allotment:**
- Maintenance and upkeep of the family vehicle (referenced in letters)
- Continued construction on the family home (referenced in letters —
  the house was still being finished more than ten years after the 1960 fire)
- General household needs ("anything else you need" — September 3, 1971 letter)
- Photographic film (referenced explicitly in September 3, 1971 letter PS —
  Marvin's mother purchased film on his behalf and presumably sent it to him)

**Context for the $10 kept:** Military Payment Certificates (MPC, called
"script" by soldiers) served as currency on US bases in Vietnam. For an
infantryman living primarily at remote fire support bases rather than rear
areas, MPC had limited practical use — the PX and other facilities where it
could be spent were largely inaccessible from the field. $10 per month was
likely sufficient for whatever small purchases were available in the field,
and Marvin appears to have had little occasion to spend more.

**Significance:** Marvin's letters home frequently reference the family
vehicle, the construction, and family members' employment — particularly
asking whether his brothers Don ("Buss") and Dan are working steady. These
are not idle questions. He was the primary outside earner for the household
during his tour, aware that his pay was what kept the car running and the
house moving toward completion. His consistent attention to domestic details
across the letters reflects this awareness.

### Marvin's Life Before Vietnam — 1967–1970

**High school graduation:** Marvin graduated from high school in 1968, Kittanning, Armstrong County, Pennsylvania.

**Electrical work near Ridgway, PA:** After graduation, Marvin took work running electrical lines near Ridgway, PA, working for the father-in-law of his oldest brother Don ("Buss"). This was physical, skilled labor — consistent with a young man from a working-class family putting his hands to practical use after school. Ridgway is in Elk County, approximately 75 miles northeast of Kittanning.

**Art Institute of Pittsburgh — Fall 1969 (one semester):** Marvin enrolled at the Art Institute of Pittsburgh in fall 1969. He completed one full semester before the draft lottery in December 1969 made it clear that continuing school would not protect him from service. He did not return for a second semester.

One semester of formal arts training — composition, drawing, design fundamentals — is enough to explain two things that appear later in his service record:

First, it explains the Colonel's request. When the unit established FSB Fontaine in late January 1971, Marvin was asked by the Colonel to draw a scaled model of the new base. That request was not made of just any soldier — it was made of a man who was known to have drafting or artistic skills. His Art Institute training, however brief, had apparently been noticed or mentioned, and it distinguished him from other soldiers in a practical way at a critical moment.

Second, it places his photographic work in a different light. His 50-60 surviving photographs from Vietnam are not the casual snapshots of someone who picked up a camera for the first time in the field. He had formal arts training. He was looking at composition, light, and subject with at least some educated eye. The photographs he took — including the careful two-shot documentation of the Huey crash on April 24, 1971 — reflect someone who understood what he was doing with a camera.

**The draft lottery and the enlistment decision — late 1969/early 1970:**
The Vietnam draft lottery was introduced in December 1969, replacing the previous system with a randomized lottery based on birth dates. Marvin's birthday, September 15, fell in the lottery at a number low enough that he could expect to be drafted.

Facing certain induction, Marvin made a calculated choice: enlist voluntarily for two years rather than wait to be drafted for three. This was a common calculation — draftees served three years, while two-year enlistments were available to volunteers. By enlisting, he accepted service on his own terms and reduced his total obligation by a year.

He enlisted March 23, 1970. He would have been 20 years old, recently withdrawn from or completing his time at the Art Institute, working in the trades, and watching his draft number come up in the lottery. The decision to enlist rather than wait was pragmatic, consistent with his character as it emerges across the letters — not dramatic, not resigned, just a clear-eyed assessment of the situation and the best available option within it.

**What this means for the profile:**
Marvin Dale Miller did not go to Vietnam because he wanted to be a soldier. He went because the country was drafting young men, his number was low, and enlisting gave him slightly more control over the terms. He left behind an unfinished house, a family that depended on his earnings, a year of art school, and a job running electrical lines. He served honorably, was decorated, came home, and never spoke of it. That arc — the art student who drew firebases for colonels and photographed helicopter crashes and sent $340 a month home to finish his family's house — is the story this archive is trying to tell.

### Military Service in the Family

**Dan Miller** (Marvin's older brother, closest in age) completed military
service in West Germany, finishing in early 1970 — approximately the same
time Marvin entered basic training at Fort Dix, New Jersey. The brothers'
service overlapped in time but not in location. Dan came home as Marvin
entered the Army.

This means the Miller family had at least two sons in military service in
the late 1960s–early 1970s, which combined with the family's economic
circumstances gives additional weight to Marvin's decision to serve and
his deliberate financial arrangements during his tour.

### The Letters

Marvin's mother kept his letters during his tour and returned them to him
when he came home in December 1971. He kept them for the rest of his life.
After his death in 2004, they passed to his son (compiler of this archive).

The letters are therefore doubly preserved — sent to Vietnam, carried home,
kept for 33 years, and now archived here. Their survival is the result of
choices made by both Marvin's mother (keeping them) and Marvin himself
(keeping them after they were returned).

Approximately 20 letters are known to exist, spanning December 1970 through
November 1971. Digitization and transcription is ongoing as of April 2026.
