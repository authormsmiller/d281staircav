# Research Account — Locating and Contacting Lt. Val Romani
## Archive ID: MDM-RESEARCH-ROMANI
## Recorded: Conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025

---

### Overview

This document records the method by which Lt. Val Romani — Cat 6, Cat Platoon CO, D/2-8 Cav — was located and persuaded to share his recollections of SGT Marvin Miller. It is preserved both as a research log and as an account worth keeping in its own right.

---

### The Problem

Marvin's son had obtained a company roster for D/2-8 Cav that included contact information for Lt. Romani. The phone number listed proved to be no good. An address was also listed, and he felt reasonably confident it was current — but could not be certain.

He had no other means of contact and no way to verify the address without trying it.

---

### The Photographs

Among Marvin Miller's personal photographs from Vietnam was a collection that included images of a man whose name was not recorded in Marvin's own labeling of the collection. Through research, Marvin's son identified this man as Lt. Val Romani.

He printed the photographs and prepared a letter to mail to the address on the roster.

To make the envelope noticeable — to give it the best possible chance of being opened rather than discarded as junk mail — he affixed a **1st Air Cavalry Division patch sticker** to the outside. Then he mailed it and waited.

---

### The Wait

He heard nothing for long enough that he was beginning to give up on a response.

Then Romani called.

---

### Romani's Account of Receiving the Letter

Romani had never talked much about the war with anyone. He remains, to this day, somewhat disaffected about the value of the war itself — a position held by a number of veterans of that conflict and one that coexists, in Romani's case, with the specific memories he went on to share.

It was his **children** who persuaded him to make the call. Romani read the letter privately and then told his children about it. It was his decision to share it with them — which means he had already determined it was significant before they became involved. Their encouragement confirmed a direction he was already leaning toward.

The thing that struck Romani most — the detail his children pressed on, and that tipped him toward calling — was the particular strangeness of receiving **photographs of yourself taken 54 years ago**, as a young man in Vietnam, sent by a total stranger. Someone he had never met, the son of one of his squad leaders, had found pictures of him in a dead man's collection, identified him, and mailed them across the country on the chance that the address was still good.

That fact — its oddness, its unexpectedness, the gap of more than half a century it represented — was what moved him.

---

### Archivist Notes

**On the methodology:**
The approach used here — roster, address, printed photographs, a recognizable insignia on the envelope — is worth documenting for anyone attempting similar research. Each element served a purpose: the roster provided the lead; the address was a calculated risk; the photographs were the substance that made the letter worth opening; the 1st Cav patch was the signal on the outside that told the recipient, before they opened it, that this was not ordinary mail. The combination worked.

**On the photographs as key:**
The photographs were the deciding factor, by Romani's own account and his children's encouragement. A letter alone, from a stranger asking about Vietnam, might reasonably have been set aside by a man who had never talked much about the war. But photographs of himself at twenty-something, taken by a man now fifty years dead, carried in a collection his son had kept and searched through — that was something different. It made the past specific and present in a way a letter of inquiry could not.

**On Romani's ambivalence:**
Romani's disaffection about the war's value is noted here without judgment and without resolution. It did not prevent him from sharing what he remembered about the men who served under him, and his assessment of Marvin Miller — documented in MDM-TESTIMONY-ROMANI — is warm and specific. A man can believe a war was not worth fighting and still remember clearly the soldiers who fought it well. Both things are true of Romani, and both belong in the record.

**On the children:**
Romani's children are the unnamed figures who made this conversation possible. They heard about the letter from their father — he chose to tell them — and encouraged him to respond. The initiative was his; theirs was the confirmation. Without them, the testimony in MDM-TESTIMONY-ROMANI likely does not exist. They do not have names in this archive, but their role does.

**On the larger pattern:**
This account is one of several in the archive where the recovery of a story depended on a chain of contingencies: a phone number that didn't work but an address that did; photographs that happened to be in Marvin's collection and happened to include Romani; a sticker on an envelope; children who happened to be present when the letter arrived. Any one of those links failing, and the conversation doesn't happen. The archive exists in part because of luck, and that is worth saying plainly.
