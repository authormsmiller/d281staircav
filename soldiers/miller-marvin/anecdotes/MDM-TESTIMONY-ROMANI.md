# Testimony — Lt. Val Romani on SGT Marvin Miller
## Source: Lt. Val Romani, Cat 6 (Cat Platoon CO)
## Archive ID: MDM-TESTIMONY-ROMANI
## Recorded: Personal conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025

---

### Source

**Lieutenant Val Romani** — Cat 6 (Platoon Commander, Cat Platoon), D/2-8 Cav. Served as Marvin Miller's platoon leader from approximately September 1971 through the end of Marvin's service in December 1971. Account shared in direct personal conversation with Marvin's son in 2024–2025. Status: Living — Follow Up.

Romani is one of the few commissioned officers in the archive who served in direct command of Marvin Miller and observed him at the squad leader level over a sustained period. His testimony is evaluative in nature — an officer's assessment of a subordinate — and carries particular weight as such.

*Note: A separate account of the circumstances under which Romani was located and contacted is to be documented — see MDM-RESEARCH-ROMANI (forthcoming).*

---

### Romani's General Assessment

Romani said he preferred **"country boys"** as his squad leaders. He considered SGT Marvin Miller one of his best.

He specifically identified Marvin's background in hunting — something Marvin had done from a young age growing up in rural Armstrong County, Pennsylvania — as translating directly and effectively into performance in the bush. The skills involved: reading terrain, moving quietly, patience, awareness of what belongs in a landscape and what does not, comfort in low light and dense cover. These were not skills the Army taught in any formal sense; they were carried in from a prior life and proved to be exactly what the jungle required.

---

### Specific Recollections

---

#### 1. Running the Wire — Navigation and Reliability

Romani stated that no matter what the conditions, Marvin **never got lost**.

Romani described ordering Marvin to run the wire — to move along the perimeter of the unit's campsite to lay or check communication wire, a task that requires navigating the full perimeter circuit and returning correctly oriented. He said Marvin performed this task with ease, even at **dusk or near dark**, quickly and efficiently.

Running wire at dusk in jungle terrain is a task that demands precise spatial awareness and the ability to maintain orientation without visual reference points. That Romani specifically recalled this, and recalled it as reliable and easy-looking, indicates it was notable enough to register — that not everyone performed it the same way.

---

#### 2. Reading the Ground — The Boot Print

On patrol, the group came across a **fresh boot print** in the jungle floor. Others in the patrol wanted to pursue further — to follow the sign.

Marvin said the print was wrong. It was **too intentional** — almost deliberately pressed into an open, visible spot on the jungle floor where it could easily be found. He also pointed out a **discarded food tin** nearby. Together, Marvin read these as deliberate lures: this was not a careless trace left by a soldier moving through. This was bait. He said it was an ambush.

The patrol paused. A **Cobra helicopter assault** was ordered on the area ahead. When the area was subsequently examined, no confirmed evidence of enemy casualties was found — but the patrol did not walk into what had been laid for them.

---

#### 3. Reading the Canopy — The Palm Branches

On a separate occasion, Marvin spotted **palm branches hanging in an unnatural way** — the geometry of the canopy wrong in a way that others had not noticed or had passed without registering.

The patrol paused again. A **small detachment went around** and approached the position from a different angle. They found evidence that a **sniper had recently occupied the position** — possibly within the past day.

The sniper was not present when they arrived. Whether he had moved on independently or had detected the patrol's hesitation and withdrawn is not known.

### Archivist Notes

**On the pattern:**
Both tactical examples Romani cited share the same underlying structure: Marvin saw something in the environment that was wrong — not dramatically wrong, but subtly wrong in a way that required a trained eye to catch — and he said so, clearly enough that the patrol stopped and acted on his reading. In both cases the assessment proved correct. In both cases the cost of not stopping would likely have been significant.

This is the same pattern documented in MDM-ANECDOTE-M60, where Marvin read the geometry of his own formation in a fraction of a second and stopped a shot that would have killed three men including himself. Romani's examples predate that incident — they come from the final months of Marvin's tour, after his promotion to E-5 on August 4, 1971 — and show the same quality operating at the environmental level rather than the tactical one. He saw the whole picture, not just the obvious part of it.

**On the hunting background:**
Romani's explicit connection between Marvin's hunting background and his performance in the field is significant. Marvin grew up in rural Armstrong County, Pennsylvania — a region where hunting was a normal part of life from a young age. The skills involved — reading sign, understanding what natural positions look like undisturbed, detecting the small wrongnesses that indicate human presence, moving in low light without losing orientation — are exactly the skills Romani identified. They were not acquired in the Army; they were brought to it.

This is worth noting not as a romantic observation but as a practical one: the Army of the Vietnam era was drawing heavily from rural communities, and the field skills those communities produced had direct military value that formal training could not easily replicate.

**On Romani's characterization — "one of his best":**
Romani said he did not have many other specific recollections beyond what he shared. That the recollections he did retain — after fifty years — were these: the navigation, the boot print, the palm branches. These were the things that stuck. That is its own form of testimony.

**On the period covered:**
Romani served as Marvin's platoon leader from approximately September 1971 through December 1971 — the final three months of Marvin's tour. This means Romani's observations cover Marvin at his most experienced: a seasoned E-5 squad leader in his eleventh and twelfth months in-country, operating with the full weight of that year behind him.
