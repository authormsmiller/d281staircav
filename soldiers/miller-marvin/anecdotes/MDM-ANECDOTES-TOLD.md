# Anecdotes — Stories Marvin Told
## Source: Marvin D. Miller, told to family before his death in 2004
## Archive ID: MDM-ANECDOTES-TOLD
## Compiled: April 2026

---

### How Marvin Talked About Vietnam

Marvin Miller did talk about his Vietnam service to his family, but within
carefully chosen limits. He shared funny anecdotes — stories that were
humanizing, absurd, or self-deprecating — without ever discussing combat,
firefights, ambushes, or the deaths of men he knew. The humor was genuine
and the stories were offered freely. The operational reality was not.

This is a common and well-documented pattern among combat veterans,
particularly those from the Vietnam era. The funny stories served several
purposes simultaneously: they acknowledged the experience without requiring
the teller to explain the weight of it; they made the men real and human
rather than abstract military figures; and they allowed a connection between
veteran and family that was honest within its chosen limits.

The stories Marvin told are as much a part of the archive as the letters
and the photographs — perhaps more so, because they are what he chose to
offer. They represent his own editorial judgment about what his service
meant and what he wanted to pass on. They should be preserved exactly as
they were told, in his voice and his framing, rather than contextualized
into the operational history they deliberately avoided.

**Note to future contributors:** If other family members remember
additional stories Marvin told — even fragments — please add them here.
These are irreplaceable and exist only in living memory.

### The Gus Angelos Shower
**Source:** Marvin Miller, told to family
**Approximate period:** 1971, location unknown — possibly FSB Silver
(where a USO show is documented in the letters) or another firebase
**People named:** Gus Angelos (Gus T. Angelos, Cat Platoon, Salt Lake
City UT — on roster)

A USO show arrived at the firebase, bringing female entertainers onto
the base — a rare and much-anticipated event for the men stationed there.
At the moment the USO girls came onto the firebase, Gus Angelos decided
that this was the ideal time to take a shower.

Whether this was deliberate comedy, genuine obliviousness to the timing,
or some mixture of the two, Marvin found it funny enough to tell for the
rest of his life.

**Archivist note:** This story documents a direct social connection
between Marvin Miller and Gus Angelos that does not otherwise appear
in the archive. It confirms they were present at the same firebase during
a USO show and that Angelos was known to Marvin well enough to be a
character in a story told at home. Angelos is currently listed as Unknown
status on the Cat Platoon roster (Salt Lake City UT, 70-71). This anecdote
should be noted in his roster record.

A USO show at FSB Silver is documented in the letters from late December
1970 / early 1971. Whether the Angelos story refers to that show or a
later one is not confirmed.

### The Ice Cream
**Source:** Marvin Miller, told to family (and/or other unit veterans)
**Period:** 1971 — specific commander not confirmed
**People named:** Company commander (identity uncertain — possibly Capt.
Bill Neal, Skipper 6 through 1971, or LTC Thomas Blagg after assuming
battalion command, or another officer in the chain)

Whenever the men returned from the bush, the company commander ensured
that ice cream was waiting for them.

Ice cream was a genuine rarity for infantry soldiers operating from remote
fire support bases in Long Khanh Province in 1971. It required refrigeration,
transport, and deliberate logistical effort by someone with the authority
and the will to make it happen. That someone consistently did — and that the
men remembered it decades later — says something about the kind of command
climate D Company operated in.

The story is not about ice cream. It is about a commander who understood
that small things, done consistently, tell men whether they are seen. Coming
back from the bush and finding something cold and sweet waiting was a
wordless communication: someone with authority thought about you while you
were out there.

**Archivist note:** The specific commander is not confirmed in this account.
The company commander through most of 1971 was Captain Bill Neal (Skipper 6),
who wrote the April 20 deposition and whose leadership is documented across
multiple sources. Captain Wolf Kutter succeeded Neal as Skipper 6 in 1971-72.
LTC Thomas Blagg commanded the battalion (Stone Mountain 6). Any of these
men could be the source of the ice cream tradition — or it may have been
a company-level practice that continued across commanders. Worth asking
Weaver, Fishell, or Kutter directly: who made the ice cream happen?

### Notes on What Was Not Said

Marvin never discussed:
- The April 20, 1971 contact north of FSB Fanning
- The April 24, 1971 Huey crash at FSB Fontaine
- The death of Bill Makowski or any other soldier he knew
- Combat, firefights, or ambushes of any kind
- His promotion to Sergeant or his role as squad leader
- The NCOC dismissal (this was known to family but apparently not
  discussed in the same casual register as the funny stories)

He came home in December 1971 and passed away in 2004 — thirty-three
years during which the operational history of his service was held
privately. His son is reconstructing that history now, in 2025-2026,
from letters, photographs, and the accounts of the men who were there.

The funny stories he told, and the silence he kept about everything else,
are both part of who he was.
