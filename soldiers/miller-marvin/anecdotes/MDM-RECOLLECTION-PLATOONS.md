# Recollection — The Platoon Names Conversation
## Source: SGT Marvin Dale Miller (primary); son's recollection
## Archive ID: MDM-RECOLLECTION-PLATOONS
## Recorded: Conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025
## Original conversation: approximately 1982–1984 (son aged 10–12)

---

### Source

**SGT Marvin Dale Miller** — the subject of this archive. This recollection is drawn from a conversation between Marvin and his son when his son was approximately 10 to 12 years old, placing it roughly in the early 1980s. It is reproduced here from his son's memory of that exchange.

This conversation is believed to be the closest Marvin Miller ever came, in any recorded conversation with his son, to describing anything bad happening in Vietnam.

---

### The Conversation

Marvin was describing the structure of D Company to his son: three platoons — **Cat**, **Range**, and **Skull**. His platoon was Cat.

His son, being a child, responded that Skull sounded the coolest, and said he would have chosen Skull.

Marvin said: **Skull was decimated by a helicopter crash.**

He did not describe the crash further. He did not give a date, name casualties, or elaborate.

His son then said he would have chosen Range instead.

Marvin said: **Range was wiped out by an ambush.**

He said nothing further. The conversation ended there.

---

### What Marvin Was Referring To

**Skull Platoon — the helicopter crash:**
Marvin was referring to a crash that occurred on **May 10, 1972** — after he had left Vietnam (his discharge was effective December 2, 1971). The crash killed most of Skull Platoon. The details of this event are known to his son through subsequent research and are documented separately in the archive. Marvin knew about it, which means news of it reached him after his return — likely through Larry Cate or another contact.

**Range Platoon — the ambush:**
Marvin was referring, at least in part, to the events of **April 20, 1971** — an engagement involving Range Platoon that is documented in the archive through Ken Weaver's account and cross-referenced in MDM-ANECDOTE-M60. Marvin's characterization — "wiped out by an ambush" — is the only direct statement he ever made about this event to his son. He was present in the unit at the time. He said nothing more.

---

### His Son's Reflection

*The following is recorded in the son's own words, as he expressed it when this memory was shared:*

Looking back, I wonder if I offended him a little by suggesting I would have wanted to be in the other platoons — the ones that were gone. And in doing so, I may have missed my only opportunity to learn more. It turned out to be exactly that: the only opportunity. He never came closer to the subject again.

---

### Archivist Notes

**On what Marvin chose to say:**
Marvin did not volunteer these facts unprompted. They emerged because his son expressed a preference — first for Skull, then for Range — and Marvin corrected each one with a single sentence. He was not telling stories. He was closing doors, gently, in a way a child might not fully register as such.

The two statements are among the most compressed pieces of testimony in this archive. Each one carries an enormous weight of event, loss, and personal proximity in a single clause. "Skull was decimated by a helicopter crash." "Range was wiped out by an ambush." He said them flatly, without elaboration, and stopped.

**On the timing:**
Marvin knew about the Skull Platoon crash despite it occurring after his departure from Vietnam. This means information about it reached him through postwar contact — most likely through Larry Cate, his only sustained postwar friendship from the unit. The fact that he carried this knowledge, and that it came to his mind immediately when his son said he would have chosen Skull, suggests it weighed on him.

**On April 20, 1971:**
Marvin's characterization of Range Platoon as having been "wiped out by an ambush" is the only statement he ever made to his son about the April 20 engagement. He was in the unit when it happened. The archive contains other accounts of that day from other sources. His own words about it are these two sentences, spoken to a child, and not repeated.

**On the missed opportunity:**
His son's reflection — that he may have inadvertently closed a conversation that could have opened further — is a common experience among the children of veterans, and worth preserving as plainly as he expressed it. It is not guilt; it is the particular grief of realizing, too late, that a door existed. The archive itself is one answer to that grief: the stories are being found by other means. But this one — whatever else Marvin might have said that evening if the conversation had turned differently — is gone.
