# Anecdote — The Claymore Incident
## Source: Larry Cate (primary); Capt. William Neal (corroborating)
## Archive ID: MDM-ANECDOTE-CLAYMORE
## Recorded: Conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025

### Sources

Lifelong friend of Marvin Miller — the only member of the unit with whom Marvin maintained a friendship after the war. Larry Cate died in 2011. ### The Anecdote

At some point during Marvin Miller's tour — most likely while he held the rank of E-4 (Specialist), prior to his promotion to E-5 on August 4, 1971 — his squad was assigned a rotation of duties that included carrying the unit's claymore mines. Each night in the field, the claymores were set out around the squad's perimeter as a defensive measure; the soldier tasked with them was responsible for carrying them throughout the day's movement.

The full load of claymores, based on Larry Cate's description, weighed approximately 75–80 pounds — carried on top of Marvin's standard combat load of weapon, ammunition, water, rations, and personal gear. Marvin Miller stood approximately 5'6" and weighed no more than 140 pounds.

After several days of watching Marvin carry this load, Larry Cate and some of the other soldiers in the squad made a decision: they would get rid of the claymores by the only means available to them. In the middle of the night, they deliberately triggered the mines, claiming they had seen movement on the perimeter.

The blast woke Captain Neal, who was in the field with the unit. He shook his RTO awake to find out what had happened. By Neal's own account, he was irritated — he suspected it was nothing — but followed all required protocols in response to the reported contact.

The mines were expended. Marvin was no longer assigned to carry them.

When Marvin's son told Neal the truth of the incident in 2025 — more than fifty years later — Neal was not surprised. He said he had always suspected the incident was not what it had been reported as, but had never had proof. He accepted the news with equanimity.

### Archivist Notes

**On the season and timing:**
Larry Cate described the conditions as very hot throughout the period when this occurred, which is consistent with the dry season in Binh Dinh / Long Khanh Province. The dry season in that region runs roughly November through April. Given that Marvin was still an E-4 (promoted to E-5 August 4, 1971), and that the "new captain" — Neal — arrived around April 13, 1971, the most likely window for this incident is **April through early August 1971**, during the transitional or dry-season period. The extreme heat described by Cate may narrow this further toward April–June 1971.

**On the weight:**
A single M18A1 Claymore mine weighs approximately 3.5 pounds. A squad typically deployed 6–10 mines on a nightly perimeter, with associated firing wire and clackers. Cate's estimate of 75–80 pounds total for the full claymore load is consistent with a full complement of mines plus wire and initiators added to an already full combat load. For a man of Marvin's size and weight, this would have been a genuinely punishing carry over multiple days in tropical heat.

**On the act itself:**
What Cate and the others did was, technically, a falsification of a contact report — a serious matter in a combat zone. They destroyed government equipment (the mines), filed a false contact report, and woke their CO in the middle of the night. That Neal suspected the truth and said nothing at the time, and that he received the confirmation fifty years later without apparent anger, speaks to the informal moral logic of small-unit life: the men had identified a problem — their squad mate was being broken by an unreasonable load — and solved it in the only way open to them. Neal, a field commander, understood this even if he couldn't sanction it.

**On Marvin's acknowledgment:**
Marvin's response when his son told him the story — "sort of remembered" — is consistent with the behavior of many combat veterans when confronted with specific incidents from their service. It may reflect genuine partial memory, deliberate understatement, or simply the way in which the event registered at the time: something happened in the night, the mines were gone, he was no longer carrying them. The reason behind it may never have been explicitly told to him by Cate or the others.

**Larry Cate as source:**
Cate was Marvin's closest postwar friend from the unit — their sustained friendship over decades suggests a relationship of trust and mutual understanding. That Cate told this story (presumably with Marvin's knowledge, or at least without his objection) suggests it was not a secret Marvin wanted kept. The detail and specificity in Cate's account — the weight, the heat, the method, the middle-of-the-night timing — are consistent with firsthand memory of a significant event.

**Capt. Neal as corroborating source:**
Neal's independent account confirms the core facts: he was present, the mines were triggered at night, he was woken by the blast, he suspected it was not a genuine contact, and he acted on the report anyway. His lack of surprise when told the truth is itself significant — it suggests the incident registered to him at the time as suspicious, and that his decision not to pursue it further was a deliberate one. This makes Neal not merely a corroborating witness but a knowing participant in the outcome: by not investigating further, he allowed the informal solution to stand.
