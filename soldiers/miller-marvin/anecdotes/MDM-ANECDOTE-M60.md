# Anecdote — The M60 Incident
## Source: Larry "Pops" Fishell, Cat Platoon, D/2-8 Cav
## Archive ID: MDM-ANECDOTE-M60
## Recorded: Conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, 2024–2025

---

### Source

**Larry Fishell** ("Pops") — Cat Platoon, D/2-8 Cav, 1971. Served as RTO (Radio Telephone Operator) to SGT Marvin Miller when Miller became squad leader following his promotion to E-5 on August 4, 1971. Contact confirmed: (989) 281-1122. Status: Active — Follow Up.

This anecdote was shared by Fishell in direct conversation with Marvin's son. It is reproduced here from memory of that conversation and should be confirmed with Fishell for accuracy of detail before publication.

### The Anecdote

On patrol at an unspecified date during Marvin's time as squad leader (August–December 1971), Marvin Miller was walking point alongside Larry Fishell (his RTO, nicknamed "Pops") and an M60 machine gunner — the three of them out front, with the remainder of the squad trailing behind.

The patrol spotted three NVA soldiers on a trail (a "trotter" — a footpath used by enemy forces). Standing orders at the time were to "light up" — engage — any enemy soldiers encountered.

As the M60 gunner brought his weapon up to fire, Marvin reached over and calmly pushed the barrel toward the ground, stopping the shot. Without raising his voice or getting on the radio, he gestured to show the gunner what he had seen:

The three point men — Marvin, Fishell, and the gunner — were out front. The remainder of the squad was strung out behind them. The three NVA soldiers were at the far end of the same line. This meant the point men were directly between the NVA and the trailing squad.

If the M60 had opened up, two things would have happened simultaneously and immediately: the point men would have been in the line of fire from the M60, and the sound of M60 fire would almost certainly have caused the trailing squad — following standing orders — to open up as well, firing forward along the same line toward the contact. The three point men would have been caught between their own M60 in front and their own squad firing from behind.

Marvin saw the geometry of the entire formation in a fraction of a second, stopped the shot with a hand on the barrel, and communicated the problem with a gesture. No radio call. No shouting.

The NVA soldiers were not engaged. The patrol continued.

### Archivist Notes

**Tactical significance:**
This anecdote documents a specific leadership quality that appears repeatedly in accounts of Marvin Miller's service — the ability to read the full geometry of a tactical situation rapidly and act on that reading without hesitation or noise.

The stakes here were higher than they might initially appear. If the M60 had fired, the problem was not simply that the point men were in the line of fire from that weapon. It was that the sound of M60 fire would immediately have caused the trailing squad — following standing orders to engage — to open up as well, firing forward along the same axis toward the contact. The three point men would have been caught in a crossfire between their own weapons: the M60 firing toward the NVA from in front, and the squad firing from behind. Three men would almost certainly have been killed by their own unit.

Marvin saw all of this — the NVA, the M60, the trailing squad, the axis of fire, the consequence — before the trigger was pulled, and stopped it with one hand and a gesture. No radio. No commands. No noise that would have triggered the very response he was preventing.

**Relationship to April 20, 1971:**
This anecdote is the most direct parallel in the archive to Ken Weaver's account of April 20, 1971 — the day Cat Platoon did not fire on NVA soldiers bathing in the creek, partly because firing risked hitting Range Platoon soldiers on the opposite bank.

On April 20, Marvin was not yet squad leader (that promotion came August 4). He was a member of the patrol, not its commander. The decision not to fire was made at the patrol leader level or above. But the tactical instinct Weaver describes — don't fire because we can't target effectively and we'll hit our own people — is the same instinct Fishell describes Marvin applying months later as squad leader.

Whether Marvin was influential in the April 20 decision, or whether he simply absorbed and internalized a lesson from that day, cannot be known. What is clear is that by the time he was leading his own squad, this was an established pattern: read the whole field, not just the target.

**If Weaver was the M60 gunner:**
If Ken Weaver is confirmed as the gunner in Fishell's story, then these two men shared this experience directly — Weaver's weapon barrel pushed down by Marvin's hand, the same man whose account of April 20 describes Cat Platoon making the same kind of assessment months earlier. That would make Weaver not just a witness to the April 20 events but a participant in the same pattern of leadership and restraint that Fishell describes. Worth asking Weaver directly whether he remembers this incident.

**Fishell as source:**
Larry Fishell — nicknamed "Pops" — served as Marvin's RTO, meaning he was physically close to Marvin during patrols — his job was to carry the radio and stay near the squad leader. He would have been present for this incident, likely within arm's reach. His account is firsthand. The uncertainty he expressed about the identity of the M60 gunner is an honest qualification, not a reason to doubt the core account.

**Language note:**
Fishell used the term "trotter" for the trail — a field term for a footpath used by enemy forces. Also sometimes called a "high-speed trail" in official reports. The NVA maintained an extensive network of such paths throughout Long Khanh Province.
