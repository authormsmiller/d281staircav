### The Photograph — Primary Source Caption

Two photographs in the Miller collection document this incident. The caption
on the first photograph reads exactly:

**"(L to R) Denny Alloway, Chieu Hoi, Bill Small, Larry Cate, Leroy Sells"**

**"Members of Cat Platoon escorting the Chieu Hoi prisoner (wrapped in
Illumination Flares parachutes) onto FSB Fontaine after he walked up
Hwy 331 to our Base to surrender."**

This caption is a complete contemporary identification. It names all four
soldiers, confirms the location as FSB Fontaine, confirms the road as
Highway 331, confirms the white material as illumination flare parachutes,
and confirms the man as a Chieu Hoi rallier who walked in voluntarily.

**Soldiers identified (L to R):**
- **Denny Alloway** — Cat Platoon, RTO. Deceased. His presence confirms
  this photograph predates August 4, 1971 (when Larry Fishell became RTO).
- **[Chieu Hoi rallier]** — blindfolded with illumination flare parachute
  fabric
- **Bill Small** — Cat Platoon. Listed on roster as William T. Small,
  Dupo, IL. Status: Closed — Contributed.
- **Larry Cate** — Cat Platoon. Close personal friend of Marvin Miller.
  Deceased 2011. Appears in Marvin's other photographs.
  **Larry Cate appears in both photographs of this incident**, confirming
  Marvin was present for the entire sequence and photographed it himself.
- **Leroy Sells** — Cat Platoon. Not currently on roster — to be added.

**The man is not a prisoner of war.** He is a Chieu Hoi rallier — a person
who surrendered voluntarily under the Republic of Vietnam's Chieu Hoi
("Open Arms") amnesty program. Coming in under Chieu Hoi was a formal act.

**The white material** is illumination flare parachute fabric repurposed
as a blindfold — standard procedure to prevent a rallier from observing
the firebase layout before being processed by intelligence.

---

### The Circumstances

**Date:** Approximately May 1971, based on accounts from unit veterans.
This is consistent with the presence of Dennis Alloway as the RTO visible
in the photograph — Alloway served before Larry Fishell took the RTO role
when Marvin became squad leader on August 4, 1971.

**Location:** Highway 331 near FSB Fontaine — confirmed by the handwritten
caption on the second photograph. This is a primary source identification
and should be treated as definitive unless contradicted by additional evidence.

**Note on FSB:** An earlier oral account suggested FSB Fanning as the likely
location. The caption on the second photograph specifies FSB Fontaine. These
are neighboring firebases in Long Khanh Province and the distinction matters
for the unit history timeline. FSB Fontaine is accepted as the correct
location based on the contemporary written source.

The rallier walked out of the jungle on his own and was moving along
Highway 331 toward the firebase when he was encountered — consistent with
the Chieu Hoi protocol, which required ralliers to present themselves to
South Vietnamese or allied forces. A group of soldiers went out from the
firebase to escort him in. The blindfold was applied before he reached the
perimeter.

---

### Chieu Hoi Program — Context

The Chieu Hoi ("Open Arms") program was established by the Republic of
Vietnam in 1963 and ran throughout the war. It offered amnesty, safety, and
resettlement assistance to NVA soldiers, Viet Cong fighters, and VC
political cadre who defected. Ralliers — called "Hoi Chanh" — were processed
by South Vietnamese intelligence and sometimes became Kit Carson Scouts
(former enemy fighters employed as guides and interpreters by US forces).

A man walking along a road toward a firebase in daylight under Chieu Hoi
terms was following the program's established protocol. He was not escaping,
not lost, and not surrendering under fire — he was presenting himself
deliberately. The encounter would have been procedural rather than combative,
though standard security precautions (the blindfold, the armed escort) still
applied.

---

### The Intelligence Officer

A man in a clean uniform is visible in the photograph, kneeling near the
rallier. This is believed to be an intelligence officer — the clean uniform
distinguishing him from the field soldiers around him. Chieu Hoi ralliers
were processed by South Vietnamese intelligence (and often US Military
Intelligence advisors), who would have interviewed the man, assessed his
intelligence value, and taken custody of his belongings for processing.

### The Physical Artifacts — Their Significance

Marvin's son possesses two objects taken from this rallier:

**1. A woodblock printing stamp ("propaganda block")**
A carved wooden block used to print propaganda materials — leaflets,
posters, or items distributed by the NVA/VC to civilians or other fighters.
The presence of this object suggests the rallier may have been involved in
political or propaganda work within the VC infrastructure rather than purely
military operations. VC political cadre were among the most common Chieu Hoi
ralliers, as they often had less combat commitment than frontline soldiers
and more exposure to the program's messaging.

**2. Black fabric**
A piece of black fabric taken from the rallier.

**Why Marvin had these objects:**
Once the rallier was in the custody of the intelligence officer, his
belongings would have been confiscated and processed formally. The fact
that Marvin came home with these objects strongly implies he was among
the group that went out to escort the rallier from the road — in the
window before formal intelligence custody began. What he took, he took
in that window.

### Archivist Notes

**The propaganda block as a historical artifact:**
Carved woodblock printing stamps used for NVA/VC propaganda are rare
surviving artifacts. The specific imagery or text on this block has not
been printed in over fifty years. Inking the block and pressing it onto
paper would recover the actual propaganda impression it was carved to
produce — a document that exists nowhere else. This should be done carefully
and documented photographically before and after.

A Vietnamese language or history specialist could potentially identify the
content, the issuing organization, and the approximate period of the block's
use. Texas Tech's Vietnam Center and Archive would be a natural referral.

**The second photograph's caption as primary source:**
The handwritten caption on the second photograph — identifying the man as
Chieu Hoi and placing the incident on Highway 331 near FSB Fontaine — was
written at or close to the time of the event, most likely by Marvin himself
or by whoever labeled the photograph. It supersedes later oral recollections
on matters of location and classification, and should be treated as the
definitive identification for archival purposes.

**Implications for the May 1971 timeline:**
If this incident occurred in May 1971, it falls in the same period as the
May 22, 1971 letter — the first letter Marvin wrote after the April 20
contact. The company had been sent to the rear after April 20 and was
returning to or operating from FSB Fontaine area by late May. A Chieu Hoi
encounter on Highway 331 in that period is consistent with the unit's
known movements.

### Source

**Ken Weaver** and other unit veterans provided context for this photograph

### The Photograph — What Is Known

A photograph in the Miller collection shows a Viet Cong prisoner in the
custody of D/2-8 Delta Company soldiers. The prisoner appears to have white
material wrapped around his head in what resembles bandaging.

**Correction of initial interpretation:** The white material is not bandaging
from wounds. It is parachute fabric from illumination flares, repurposed as
a blindfold. The purpose was to prevent the prisoner from seeing the layout
of the firebase as he was brought in — standard procedure to deny him
intelligence he could report if he escaped or was released.

---

### The Circumstances

The prisoner walked out of the jungle on his own and was moving along a road
heading toward the firebase when he was encountered. He was not captured in
a firefight or raid.

A group of soldiers went out from the firebase to meet him and bring him in.
The blindfold was applied before he reached the perimeter — again, to prevent
him from observing the firebase's layout, positions, and defenses.

**Location:** Most likely FSB Fanning, based on the presence of Dennis Alloway
as the RTO visible in the photograph. Alloway served as RTO before Larry
Fishell took the role when Marvin became squad leader on August 4, 1971.
This dates the photograph to before August 1971 — most likely spring or
early summer 1971, during the period when the company was at or near FSB
Fanning.

**Date:** Unknown precisely. Before August 1971. Possibly April–July 1971.
FSB Fanning is the most probable location based on the RTO identification.

---

### The Intelligence Officer

A man in a clean uniform is visible in the photograph, kneeling near the
prisoner. This is believed to be an intelligence officer — the clean uniform
distinguishing him from the field soldiers around him, who would have been
in worn, dirty fatigues. Once a prisoner was in the custody of an
intelligence officer, his personal effects and belongings would typically
have been confiscated and processed as intelligence materials.

---

### The Physical Artifacts — Their Significance

Marvin's son possesses two objects taken from this prisoner:

**1. A woodblock printing stamp ("propaganda block")**
A carved wooden block used to print propaganda materials — leaflets,
posters, or other printed items distributed by the NVA/VC to civilians
or enemy forces. The presence of this object on the prisoner suggests
he may have been involved in political or propaganda work for the NVA/VC
infrastructure, not purely military operations.

**2. Black fabric**
A piece of black fabric taken from the prisoner. Black fabric (or black
pajamas) was strongly associated with Viet Cong fighters and local
guerrillas in the popular imagination of the period, though in practice
black clothing was common among Vietnamese civilians as well.

**Why these objects are significant:**
Once the prisoner was in the custody of the intelligence officer, his
possessions would ordinarily have been confiscated and processed as
intelligence materials — they would not have been available for individual
soldiers to keep as souvenirs. The fact that Marvin came home with these
two objects strongly implies he was among the group that went out to meet
the prisoner before he reached the firebase perimeter and before
intelligence custody began — in the window between initial encounter and
formal processing.

This is consistent with his role in the unit and with his pattern of
careful documentation: a soldier who photographed what he saw, and who
in this case was present at the encounter itself.

### Archivist Notes

**The propaganda block as a historical artifact:**
Carved woodblock printing stamps used for NVA/VC propaganda are rare
surviving artifacts from the conflict. Most were destroyed, lost, or
never preserved. The specific imagery or text on this block — whatever
it was designed to print — could potentially be identified by a
Vietnamese language specialist or a curator with expertise in the period's
propaganda materials. This object may have independent historical value
beyond its connection to Marvin's service.

**Identification questions still open:**

- The intelligence officer in the clean uniform — unidentified. Could
  potentially be identified by unit veterans who remember the incident.
- Other soldiers visible in the photograph — to be identified with
  Weaver's assistance and that of other veterans.

**The prisoner himself:**
Unknown. Walking alone on a road toward a firebase — whether he was
surrendering, lost, or attempting something else is not recorded. His
fate after intelligence processing is unknown.

**Dating implications for the photograph archive:**
If this photograph dates to before August 1971 and most likely to the
FSB Fanning period (approximately April–July 1971), it places it in the
same operational window as the April 20 contact and the April 24 Huey
crash. It may be among the photographs taken in the weeks or months
following those events, during the period when the company was at or
near Fanning.
