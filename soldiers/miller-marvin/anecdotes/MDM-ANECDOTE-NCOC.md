# Anecdote — The NCOC Dismissal
## Source: Marvin D. Miller (told to family); daughter's recollection
## Archive ID: MDM-ANECDOTE-NCOC
## Recorded: Family knowledge, compiled April 2026

---

### Source and Reliability

This account comes from two sources within the family:

- **Marvin Miller himself** — told the broad outline of the story to family
  at some point before his death in 2004, though he never disclosed the
  specific infraction.
- **Marvin's daughter** — believed the infraction involved marijuana, and
  that Marvin refused to identify who was smoking it.

Marvin's characteristic reticence about his service means this is one of
the few pre-Vietnam stories he apparently shared, possibly because it had
a satisfying ironic conclusion. The details are therefore more reliable
than many family anecdotes — he told it himself, and he found it worth
telling.

---

### The Account

During the NCO Candidate Course (NCOC) at Fort Benning, Georgia —
approximately August–October 1970 — Marvin was accused by a captain of
an infraction serious enough to warrant dismissal from the program.

The specific infraction was never disclosed by Marvin to his family.
His sister believed he had been found in the presence of marijuana and
refused to identify who was actually smoking it. Whether this was the
precise nature of the charge or a family interpretation is not confirmed.
What is consistent with Marvin's character across every account in the
archive — from the M60 incident to the letters home — is the refusal to
give up another soldier. Protecting the man next to him, or behind him,
was a pattern, not an exception.

He was called into the Colonel's office — believed to be the commanding
officer of the NCOC program — to be informed of his dismissal.

The Colonel told him two things. First, that he did not like the captain
who had made the referral and did not believe him regarding Marvin's
personal involvement. Second, that it did not matter: it was a captain's
word against a PFC's, and the PFC was always going to lose. Marvin was
dismissed from the program.

The captain, in dismissing him, told Marvin that he would remain a PFC
and never be promoted.

**Less than a year later, on August 4, 1971, Marvin Dale Miller was
promoted to Sergeant E-5 in the field in Vietnam.**

He found this ironic. He said so.

---

### Archivist Notes

**The charge and the character:**
Marvin never told his family what exactly he was accused of. The refusal
to name names — if his daughter's interpretation is correct — is entirely
consistent with every other account of him in this archive. In the M60
incident, he stopped a shot to protect his own point men without making
a sound. In the April 20 contact, Cat Platoon held fire rather than risk
hitting Range soldiers on the opposite bank. The pattern in every case is
the same: he read the full picture, made a quiet decision, and did not
sacrifice someone else to protect himself.

Whether the marijuana interpretation is precisely correct or not, the
core of the story — that he knew something, refused to say it, and took
the consequences alone — is consistent with who he was.

**The Colonel's candor:**
The Colonel's statement is worth noting on its own terms. He told a PFC,
directly, that he didn't believe the captain, and that it didn't matter
because rank determined outcome regardless of truth. That is a remarkably
honest thing for a commanding officer to say to an enlisted man he was
dismissing. It suggests the Colonel wanted Marvin to understand that the
system, not the evidence, was working against him — which is a different
thing than saying he was guilty.

**The captain's prediction and its irony:**
"You'll remain a PFC and never be promoted."

Marvin was promoted to E-5 — Sergeant — on August 4, 1971, in the field
in Vietnam, less than a year after that conversation. He became, by every
account in the archive, an effective and respected squad leader whose men
trusted him with their lives. Larry Fishell rode as his RTO and told the
story of the M60 incident decades later. Ken Weaver remembered him.

The captain was wrong on both counts: the rank and the character.

Marvin found this funny. The fact that he told the story at all — this
man who came home and never spoke about Vietnam — suggests the irony
mattered to him. It was one of the few moments from that period he
apparently chose to share.

**Timeline implications:**
The NCOC dismissal is the event that sent Marvin to Fort Hood rather than
to Vietnam as a newly minted Sergeant. Had he completed the NCOC, he would
have arrived in Vietnam as an E-5 in a different unit, at a different time,
possibly in a different role. The dismissal — unjust on the Colonel's own
assessment — is another point in the chain of contingencies that placed
Marvin Miller, specifically, in Cat Platoon, D/2-8 Cav, on April 20, 1971,
and at FSB Fontaine on April 24, 1971.

**The promotion as closure:**
The E-5 promotion came through normal channels in the field — not through
the NCOC, but through demonstrated performance as a soldier and squad
leader. He earned the rank the captain said he would never have, in the
place where it was hardest to earn it, by doing the job in front of people
who could see him do it.

The archive does not record what became of the captain.
