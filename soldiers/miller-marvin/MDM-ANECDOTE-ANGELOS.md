# Anecdote — Gus Angelos and the USO Truck
## Source: SGT Marvin Dale Miller (primary)
## Archive ID: MDM-ANECDOTE-ANGELOS
## Recorded: Conversation with son of SGT Marvin Miller, date unknown (prior to Dec 4, 2005)

---

### Source

**SGT Marvin Dale Miller** — the subject of this archive. This anecdote is one of the few stories Marvin told directly about his service. It was shared with his son at an unspecified date prior to Marvin's death on December 4, 2005. It is reproduced here from his son's memory of that conversation.

Unlike most accounts in this archive, which come from fellow soldiers located after Marvin's death, this one comes from Marvin himself — in his own words, as his son remembers them.

---

### Context Marvin Provided

Marvin told this story in the context of describing conditions on a firebase — specifically the absence of privacy and the scarcity of any comfort or luxury. He described the shower setup: a tripod erected near the road at the entrance to the firebase, with a black rubber bladder hanging from it to hold water. There was no enclosure, no curtain, nothing to shield the bather from view. Soldiers showered in the open, in front of anyone who happened to be passing.

---

### The Anecdote

A soldier in the unit named **Gus Angelos** — described by Marvin as "crazy" or "wild," meaning he had a large, outgoing personality — was apparently alert to opportunity.

On one occasion, word got around that a truck carrying girls from a USO show was coming through the entrance of the firebase. Angelos made his decision immediately: he walked to the tripod shower by the road and began showering, in full view of the entrance, precisely as the truck came in.

Marvin did not say whether anything came of it — no conversation, no reaction from the girls recorded. The story ends with the image of Angelos, naked under the rubber bladder, positioned at the road, as the truck went by. The memory Marvin carried was not of any outcome, but of the decision itself: the timing, the deliberateness, the complete absence of self-consciousness.

---

### Archivist Notes

**On Marvin as source:**
This is among the small number of stories Marvin Miller chose to tell about his service. He was generally reticent about Vietnam, and the stories he did tell tend to illuminate character — his own or someone else's — rather than action or danger. That he remembered and retold this one suggests Angelos made a strong impression on him.

**On the story's context:**
Marvin framed this story as an illustration of firebase conditions — no privacy, no amenities, life conducted in the open. The shower detail is itself useful historical texture: the field expedient shower (rubber bladder on a tripod, positioned near a road for access) was common across firebases in Vietnam, and Marvin's description is consistent with accounts from other veterans of the period. The story arrives embedded in that context, which suggests Marvin was making a point about what life was actually like before the Angelos detail landed as the punchline.

**On Gus Angelos:**
Marvin's characterization — "crazy" or "wild," meaning a large personality rather than dangerous or unstable — suggests Angelos was known in the unit as someone who went his own way, possibly a source of morale through sheer force of character. The name is distinctive enough that it may be traceable through unit rosters. His role and rank in the unit are not known from this account.

**On tone:**
This is a comedy. Marvin told it as one. It belongs in the archive alongside the heavier accounts not in spite of that, but because of it — it is evidence that there was laughter, that men found ways to be ridiculous, and that fifty years later a soldier's son could still hear his father's amusement in the telling.
