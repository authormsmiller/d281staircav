### Transcription

3 Sept 71

Dear Mom,

Well here it is September now. The days keep chugging by. I don't like to think about it too much, the time doesn't go as fast then if you're thinking about it all the time. I guess my birthday this month too.

The weather really changed over here. We had it pretty nice for awhile but it really changed about a week ago. That one day it rained so hard I just couldn't believe it. It might be changing again now. Last night was clear and so is today so far.

We moved to another base. It's called Jeffreys. It's not very far from a Vietnamese village.

Well there sure isn't much to write about, but I thought I better write to let you know I'm OK. Hope [crossed out word] everyone is feeling alright back home too. Maybe I'll be able to think of more to write tomorrow.

Love
Marv.

P.S. I didn't know if I had any money left or not. So go ahead and use it for the film or anything else you need.

---

### Transcription Notes
- Page 2, line 4: "Hope [crossed out word]" — Marvin wrote a word then crossed it out before writing "everyone is feeling alright." The crossed-out word appears to be "all" or possibly "an" — he likely started "Hope all" or "Hope everyone" and corrected himself mid-sentence. The strikethrough is deliberate.
- "Jeffreys" — Marvin spells the FSB name as "Jeffreys" (with a y). This is the first primary source confirmation of the spelling. Cross-reference: we previously identified this FSB as named for WO1 Gabriel A. Jeffries Jr. (killed April 24, 1971 Huey crash) whose surname appears on the Vietnam Memorial Wall as "Jeffries" with an i. The discrepancy between Marvin's "Jeffreys" and the Wall's "Jeffries" is a minor variant worth noting — informal FSB names were often spelled phonetically by different soldiers.
- "the film" in the PS — refers to photographic film, consistent with Marvin being an active photographer during his tour. He maintained a balance of money that his mother had access to, and is authorizing her to use it.
- All other text is clearly legible.

### Archivist Notes

**Date context:**
September 3, 1971 — this letter is the direct primary source confirmation of the movement timeline entry for that date: *"3 Sep 1971 — Moved to FSB Jeffreys, not far from a Vietnamese village."* Previously that entry was derived from this letter; the letter now stands as the source document behind it. Marvin had approximately three months remaining in-country when he wrote this (DEROS December 2, 1971).

**Recent promotion — SGT E-5:**
As of September 3, 1971, Marvin had been a Sergeant for exactly 30 days. His DD-214 records the date of rank as August 4, 1971 — confirmed. The promotion came during or immediately following a rotation of personnel in July 1971, during which new leadership arrived including Dale Tincher (Battalion S-1, arriving approximately August 1971) and Val Romani (Cat 6 — Cat Platoon leader, arriving late 1971, with a brief overlap with Tincher). The promotion to E-5 placed Marvin in a squad leader role, which is consistent with Larry Fishell's confirmation that he served as Marvin's RTO "when he was a squad leader" — Fishell would have come into this role around or after the August 4 promotion.

Marvin does not mention the promotion in this letter. He had been a Sergeant for a month when he wrote it.

**Personnel context — July 1971 rotation:**
Based on accounts from Dale Tincher and Val Romani (confirmed contacts), a rotation of personnel occurred in July 1971. This is consistent with the broader Vietnamization drawdown — units were receiving new personnel and transitioning leadership as the US presence contracted. Wayne Robinson, listed as Marvin's squad leader in April 1971, may have rotated out during this period, which would have opened the squad leader position Marvin subsequently filled upon his E-5 promotion in August.
Marvin notes "I guess my birthday this month too" — with characteristic understatement, almost as an afterthought. SGT Marvin Dale Miller was born September 15, 1949. He turned 22 years old on September 15, 1971, twelve days after writing this letter, while stationed at FSB Jeffreys in Long Khanh Province, Vietnam. He would be home by December 2.

**Short-timer psychology:**
The opening paragraph is the most psychologically revealing passage of any letter in the collection so far. "The days keep chugging by. I don't like to think about it too much, the time doesn't go as fast then if you're thinking about it all the time." This is a precise description of the short-timer's cognitive strategy — deliberately not counting the days, because counting makes the waiting unbearable. It's a soldier who knows he's close to going home and is managing that awareness carefully. The later November 24 letter will describe this explicitly as a "short-timer's attitude," but here in September it is already present, undeclared.

**FSB Jeffreys — confirmed and named:**
This letter is the primary source document establishing that FSB Jeffreys existed and that Delta Company was there on September 3, 1971. Two details from the letter are archivally significant:

First, the spelling: Marvin writes "Jeffreys" — this is how the name sounded to him and how he chose to render it. WO1 Gabriel A. Jeffries Jr., for whom the base was almost certainly named, has his surname recorded as "Jeffries" on the Vietnam Veterans Memorial Wall. The variant spelling in this letter is not an error but a natural phonetic rendering by a soldier who heard the name rather than read it — consistent with how FSB names circulated verbally in the field.

Second, the proximity to a Vietnamese village: "It's not very far from a Vietnamese village." This detail has potential geographic value — FSB locations near villages can sometimes be identified on period maps using village locations as reference points. If the base can be located geographically, this detail helps.

**The PS and the money — financial arrangement:**
The postscript reveals a domestic financial arrangement that appears to have been consistent throughout Marvin's tour. He kept approximately $10 per month for personal use and sent the remainder of his pay home to his mother, who managed it on his behalf. He writes "I didn't know if I had any money left or not" because he was not managing the balance himself — his mother held it.

For context: an E-4 infantryman in Vietnam in 1971 earned approximately $288 base pay per month, plus combat pay, hazardous duty pay, and overseas allowances — totaling roughly $350–400 per month. If Marvin kept $10 and sent the rest, he was sending his mother approximately $340 per month. For a family in Kittanning, Pennsylvania in 1971, this was a meaningful contribution.

The "script" referenced by other unit members was Military Payment Certificates (MPC) — the currency used by US forces in Vietnam instead of American dollars, specifically to prevent US currency from entering the local economy or black market. MPC could be used at the PX, mess hall, and on-base services, but had limited practical use for an infantryman living in the field at a fire support base. Marvin's pattern of keeping only $10 suggests he found little to spend money on in the field — consistent with spending most of his tour at remote FSBs rather than in rear areas where the PX and other facilities were accessible.

He explicitly names "film" as the first suggested use for the money — photographic film, consistent with his being an active photographer during his tour. His mother was apparently his supply channel for film, purchasing it with his allotted funds and presumably sending it to him in the field.
