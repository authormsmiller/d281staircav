### Transcription

24 Aug 71

Dear Mom,

Well, here I am again. I haven't been too busy lately so I have time to write more. But there isn't much to write about.

It's been pretty cool over here lately and it hasn't rained in the last 3 or 4 days. Hope you're having nice weather back home. It makes the days a little brighter.

I'm going to start sending my film to a different firm. It would cost about half as much to develope them there. The only trouble is they don't except COD's. Which means I'll have to get some money orders or if I can't get that, you'll have to send me some greenbacks. Maybe I can get the money orders if I go back to the rear (Bien Hoa).

Well's how's everybody back home. I haven't been getting too much mail lately. I get one from you about every three days tho. Mothers are about the only really consistent writers we fellows have over here. It's usually the first letter we read too.

Well it's getting too dark to write anymore

Love
Marv

---

### Transcription Notes
- Marvin spells "develop" as "develope" and "accept" as "except" and "too" as "to" in places — these are his natural spelling and grammar, preserved exactly as written. Not errors to correct but authentic voice.
- Page 1 top: faint mirror-writing visible through the page from a previous letter in the same envelope or stack — not part of this letter.
- "Mothers are about the only really consistent writers we fellows have over here" — the word "have" appears written above the line as an insertion, added after he wrote the sentence and realized he'd omitted it. Shows him re-reading as he wrote.
- Letter ends mid-close — "Well it's getting too dark to write anymore / Love / Marv" — suggesting he was writing outdoors or by natural light and the day was ending.

### Archivist Notes

**Date context and sequencing:**
August 24, 1971 — this letter was written eleven days before the September 3 letter (MDM-LETTER-19710903) and exactly twenty days after his promotion to E-5 on August 4. This is the earliest letter in the current archive that postdates the promotion, though he does not mention it here either.

The letter sequence now established is:
- May 22, 1971 — first letter after April 20 contact
- August 24, 1971 — this letter (11 days before September 3)
- September 3, 1971 — FSB Jeffreys letter

There is a gap of approximately three months between May 22 and August 24. Whether letters were written during this period and lost, or whether Marvin wrote less frequently during the summer months, is unknown. The July 1971 personnel rotation, his promotion on August 4, and the transition to a new role as squad leader may have affected his writing rhythm.

**"I haven't been too busy lately" — operational tempo:**
This is a notable phrase in context. August 1971 was a period of reduced operational activity for many US units as Vietnamization accelerated and American forces drew down. The fact that Marvin was "not too busy" in late August may reflect the broader operational picture — fewer patrols, less field time, more base time — rather than anything specific to his platoon. The September 3 letter confirms the move to FSB Jeffreys happened around this period, and a base transition often meant a period of settling in with reduced patrol tempo.

**The photography logistics — most detailed account yet:**
This letter contains the most specific information in the collection about Marvin's photography operation, and it clarifies the financial arrangement from the September 3 PS considerably.

He was sending exposed film to a commercial photo developing firm for processing. He had been using one firm and was switching to a cheaper one — "about half as much to develop them there." The problem: the new firm did not accept COD (Cash on Delivery) payments, which was how he had apparently been paying the first firm. His proposed solutions, in order:

1. Get money orders — possible if he could access Bien Hoa (the rear)
2. Have his mother send him US dollars ("greenbacks") directly

"Greenbacks" is significant. US paper currency was officially prohibited in Vietnam under the MPC system — soldiers were supposed to convert dollars to MPC upon arrival. Marvin asking his mother to send him actual US dollars if necessary suggests either that this rule was loosely enforced in practice, or that he was aware of the workaround and comfortable using it. The fact that he raises it as a fallback option rather than a first choice suggests he knew it was slightly irregular.

The word "greenbacks" also confirms something important about the financial arrangement established in previous letters: the money his mother was managing on his behalf was US dollars, not MPC. His pay allotment was converted to and held in dollars at home, which is why she could potentially send him some if needed.

**The photography scale this implies:**
Switching firms to save money suggests Marvin was developing film regularly enough that the cost differential mattered. If he was saving "about half as much" by switching, and if this was worth the logistical complications of money orders versus COD, he was processing a meaningful volume of film. This is consistent with the 50-60 photographs from his tour that survive — he was an active, systematic photographer, not an occasional one.

**Bien Hoa as the rear:**
"Maybe I can get the money orders if I go back to the rear (Bien Hoa)" — he parenthetically identifies Bien Hoa as "the rear," which is consistent with the unit's operational picture. Bien Hoa was the battalion's administrative base, where the replacement depot processed new arrivals (as Marvin himself had been processed in December 1970) and where rear-echelon facilities including finance offices and postal services were available. The fact that he says "if I go back" rather than "when I go back" suggests access to Bien Hoa was irregular and not guaranteed from the field.

**"Mothers are about the only really consistent writers":**
This passage is one of the most direct and unguarded observations in the letters so far. Marvin was getting a letter from his mother approximately every three days — roughly ten letters per month, sustained across his entire tour. He notes that mothers were uniquely reliable in this regard compared to other correspondents, and adds: "It's usually the first letter we read too."

This is written in the plural — "we fellows," "we read." He's speaking for a group of men sharing an experience of mail call, comparing notes on who writes and who doesn't. It suggests a collective awareness among soldiers of whose families wrote and how often. Letters from home were a communal morale resource as much as a personal one — men shared news, talked about what they'd heard, read passages aloud. His mother writing every three days placed her among the most attentive correspondents in the platoon.

It also quietly honors her. He's telling her, in the understated way he tells her everything, that what she's been doing matters. That her letters arrive first at mail call. That they get read first.
